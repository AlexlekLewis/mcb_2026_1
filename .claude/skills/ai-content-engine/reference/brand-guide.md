## 1. Brand essence

> Modern Curtains and Blinds is the **honest local installer Melbourne
> homeowners wish they'd found years ago** — design-aware enough to
> respect their house, pragmatic enough to tell them when something won't
> work, and around long enough that their neighbour's already seen the
> ute on the street.

That's the line. Every piece of content should ladder up to it.

---

## 2. Positioning

What MCB IS:

- A family-owned, Preston-based, Melbourne-wide window furnishings installer.
- The "we'll come and measure, bring samples, give you a real quote" service.
- Quietly design-aware — comfortable with Federation, Edwardian, mid-century, contemporary and architect-designed homes.
- Honest about trade-offs (every product has a downside; we'll tell you which one matters).

What MCB is NOT:

- Not the cheapest. Don't compete on price; compete on getting it right the first time.
- Not the most premium / "luxury" brand. We're not a Hunter Douglas showroom or an interior-design studio.
- Not a national chain. We're not "Melbourne's #1" — that's a thing chains say.
- Not a marketing operation. The site is the installer talking, not a copywriter performing.
- Not a DIY content site. We don't write 2,000-word listicles. We answer the question, well.

The competitive position in one sentence: **"the local who's done your neighbourhood before, takes design seriously, and tells the truth about what your windows actually need."**

---

## 3. Personality / archetype

Two archetypes blended:

- **The Sage** (60%) — the one with the expertise. We've measured up enough homes to know what works. We share what we know plainly.
- **The Helper** (40%) — the one who solves it. We come to you. We bring the samples. We make the call easier, not harder.

We're NOT:

- The Hero (chest-thumping, "we're the best", aspirational achievement).
- The Magician (mystery, transformation, lifestyle-fantasy copy).
- The Outlaw (rule-breaking, edgy, contrarian for its own sake).
- The Innocent (folksy, twee, "since 1947" nostalgia copy).

## 4. Voice attributes (the paired-adjective test)

When you're drafting and want to know "is this on-brand?", check it lands inside each pair:

| More this | …not this |
|---|---|
| **Warm** | …chummy or fake-mate. ("g'day", "mate", "champion" — never.) |
| **Expert** | …showy. We don't lecture. We don't list credentials in body copy. |
| **Honest** | …blunt to the point of cold. Hedge where conditional. |
| **Opinionated** | …dogmatic. We take a position; we don't insist on it. |
| **Local** | …parochial. Melbourne-specific yes; bagging-other-suburbs no. |
| **Practical** | …joyless. We can be dry-funny. We don't optimise for laughs. |
| **Tidy** | …clinical. Variable sentence length; the odd aside is fine. |
| **Australian** | …forced-Australian. No "fair dinkum", no "true blue", no slang as performance. |

**The kitchen-table test:** every sentence should be something the owner could actually say to a customer standing at their kitchen table with a fabric sample. If it can't survive that test, it can't survive the page.

## 5. Tone matrix — when to slide which way

Voice is constant; tone shifts by context.

| Page context | Slide toward |
|---|---|
| Pricing / how-it-works questions | More honest, more practical. Let the trade-off do the work. |
| Product-comparison questions | More opinionated, more expert. Take the position. |
| Aesthetic / "what suits my home" questions | More design-aware. Reference housing eras, fabrics, light. |
| Functional questions (heat, light, privacy) | More technical, more numeric. Give the mechanism. |
| Maintenance / "what if it breaks" questions | More patient, more reassuring. Less salesy. |
| Anything legal / regulatory | We don't write this. Route to Alex. |

## 6. Vocabulary — words that are us, words that aren't

### Yes — these are us

- **We / we'd / we'll / we've / our team / we install / we measure / we recommend**
- **The free in-home measure and quote** (the offer phrase — always include "in-home" so it's clear we come to you)
- **Free measure-up** (informal alternative — fine in short text links)
- **Around / round the corner / out the back / the rear extension / the front room**
- **Most weeks / last month / earlier this year** (anecdote markers — concrete, not "in our experience")
- **Wavefold** (preferred over S-fold / ripplefold for SEO consistency — both are correct, MCB uses Wavefold)
- **Blockout** (never "blackout")
- **Curtains / blinds / shutters / security screens / awnings** (product nouns — never "shades", "drapes", "window treatments")
- **Plantation shutters** (full phrase — never just "shutters" when we mean plantation)
- **Basswood, PVC, aluminium, timber** (material names — concrete)
- **Honeycomb / cellular blinds, sunscreen, blockout, sheer, double roller** (fabric/system nouns)
- **Suburb names from `voice-anchors.ts`** — always pick a specific one when illustrating a point
- **"Honest answer is" / "the bottom line" / "the trade-off is"** (truth-marker phrases)

### No — these aren't us

Marketing-speak:
- "premium", "luxurious" (without context), "bespoke", "tailored to you", "the perfect choice"
- "leading", "premier", "trusted", "award-winning" (without naming the award)
- "Melbourne's #1", "Australia's best", "voted by [vague body]"
- "Transform your home", "elevate your space", "unlock the potential"
- "Step into a world of", "experience the difference"
- "Look no further", "we've got you covered", "where style meets function"

Forced-Australian:
- "g'day", "mate", "true blue", "fair dinkum", "ridgy-didge", "no worries"
- "arvo", "barbie", "tradie" (when referring to ourselves — fine for context)

American imports:
- "shades" (use "blinds"), "drapes" (use "curtains"), "window treatments" (use the actual product name)
- "reach out", "circle back", "touch base", "ballpark" (US idiom)
- "story" (for floor of a building — use "storey")
- "zip code" (use "postcode")
- "color", "organize", "customize" (US spelling)

Phrases that imply we don't know our customer:
- "Whether you're X, Y, or Z…" (lazy opener, casts too wide)
- "Looking for…?" (rhetorical question opener)
- "In today's fast-paced world…" (literally the canonical AI-tell)

## 7. What we always do

These are the visible-in-copy commitments. Every multi-sentence answer should imply at least one of them.

- **Free in-home measure and quote.** We come to you. No fee. No obligation.
- **We bring samples** — fabric, colour, finish, where relevant.
- **We measure every opening** that's in scope for the visit.
- **Written quote before anything is ordered.** Clear, line-itemed, no surprises.
- **Multi-product quotes in one visit.** Curtains, blinds, shutters, screens, awnings, motorisation — all assessable in one appointment.
- **We tell you when something won't work.** Bathroom timber, fragile reveal, body-corporate restrictions on external awnings — we say so.

---

## 8. What we never do

Hard rules — gate-enforced where possible, otherwise vigilance:

- **Never quote a specific price in published copy.** Every window's different. We answer pricing questions with the *honest broker* pattern — explain what affects the price, name the trade-offs, point to the free quote — but no per-window numbers in body copy. (See `service-no-published-pricing` in `approved-facts.ts`.)
- **Never make a claim about Victorian tenancy law, electrical regulations, fire ratings, or building codes.** Not even an obvious-true one. Route to Alex.
- **Never make a superlative claim ("best", "leading", "#1") without naming the source and year.** "Voted Best by Houzz 2023" = fine. "Melbourne's leading installer" = banned.
- **Never use a stock photo to illustrate a product or installation.** Real installs only. If we don't have one, we skip the visual rather than fake it.
- **Never imply scarcity or urgency we don't have.** No "limited time", no "only 3 left", no "book now or miss out".
- **Never bag a competitor by name.** We let the comparison answer for us.
- **Never use someone else's quote / endorsement without their consent.** Even a friendly review.
- **Never write copy that reads like content marketing.** No 1500-word "Ultimate Guide" articles. The 40-60 word capsule is the unit.

## 9. The customer in our head

When the skill is choosing words, picture this person:

> Sarah, 38, lives in a 1920s weatherboard in Northcote with her partner and two primary-school-age kids. They're partway through replacing the original window furnishings — half the house had op-shop curtains and aluminium Venetians the previous owner left behind. She works full-time, has thought about it for months, can tell the difference between basswood and PVC after some Pinterest research, but doesn't trust any single online price and doesn't want to be sold to. She'd rather pay 15% more for someone who turns up when they said they would. She types her questions into Google starting with "best" or "should I" or "how do I tell if". She's the reason we never lie and never inflate.

Other customers exist (renters, downsizers, design-led renovators in Brighton, families in Mill Park, body-corp committees), but Sarah is the default. When in doubt, write for Sarah.

## 10. Visual identity at-a-glance

The skill mostly writes copy, not UI — but it places copy inside the brand's visual context, so it has to know the surroundings.

### Palette

Sourced from `src/app/globals.css`. Warm, earthy, calm. Premium-but-honest. **Never** swap for high-saturation colours.

| Token | Hex | Use |
|---|---|---|
| `--color-mcb-paper` | `#F9F8F6` | Default body background. Calm off-white. |
| `--color-mcb-charcoal` | `#2D2D2D` | Default text. Not pure black. |
| `--color-mcb-terracotta` | `#B26E2D` | Accent — chevrons, in-text links, brand-coloured details. |
| `--color-mcb-terracotta-deep` | `#8E5520` | CTA buttons (AA contrast on white). |
| `--color-mcb-sand` | `#F3EFE6` | Subtle card / hover backgrounds. |
| `--color-mcb-sand-deep` | `#E8E2D7` | Borders, table dividers. |
| `--color-mcb-clay` | `#C69C85` | Warm accent — info chips, secondary illustrations. |
| `--color-mcb-sage` | `#94A385` / `dark #748B69` | Positive state — citation pass, lead success. |
| `--color-mcb-warm-grey` | `#6B6457` | Muted text, axis labels, byline. |
| `--color-mcb-terracotta-red` | `#B23A2D` | Critical state only — never decorative. |

### Type

- **Sans:** Inter (`--font-inter`) — body, UI.
- **Serif:** Playfair (`--font-playfair`) — headings, brand voice.
- **Case: sentence case throughout.** Both body and headings render in whatever case the writer typed. The site DOES NOT use `text-transform: capitalize` — that CSS rule was removed 2026-05-24 because it title-cased every word and mangled proper nouns ("PVC" → "Pvc"), conjunctions ("vs" → "Vs"), and abbreviations ("AI" → "Ai"). Never reintroduce it. When a heading needs visual emphasis use Playfair display weight or size; never force title case via CSS.
- Tabular numerals for any numbers in tables / data.
- Italic is used sparingly — for asides and emphasis only, not whole paragraphs.

### Photography rules (relevant when picking install photos for InlineAnswer)

- Real installations only. No stock.
- Honest light. Daylight, no heavy filters.
- Show the window in context — the room around it, not just the product.
- People are fine; they should be ours or our customers (with permission), not models.
- If we don't have a real photo for the topic, skip the photo — never substitute stock.

### Tone of UI copy (not body copy)

- Buttons: short, verb-led, sentence-case. "Get a Free Quote" (canonical). "Book a Visit". "Send Your Details".
- Microcopy under buttons: practical reassurance. "Free in-home measure. No obligation. ~30-second form."
- Form errors: friendly + specific. "Looks like the postcode's missing — we use it to find your nearest installer."
- Empty states: honest about what's happening. Never "nothing to see here".

## 11. Brand voice — six tuning-fork sentences

Use these as concrete tuning forks while drafting. They're shorter than the `voice-anchors.ts` paragraphs but the same DNA.

1. *"We mostly stop recommending timber shutters in bathrooms after a couple of summers of warping — PVC isn't quite as warm but it'll still be straight in ten years."*

2. *"On a west-facing window in Brighton we'd usually pair a honeycomb blockout with an external folding-arm awning before doing anything fancier — stop the heat at the glass, not after it's already inside."*

3. *"The honest answer is that wavefold curtains look right on tall windows and a bit busy on short ones — if your ceiling is under 2.4m we'd suggest a softer pinch pleat instead."*

4. *"Most weeks we measure up at least one Reservoir brick veneer where the previous owner's vertical blinds have given up — we usually replace them with panel glides on the same track."*

5. *"Sheer curtains give you daytime privacy and not much at night with the lights on — if the lounge faces the street, we'd run a blockout layer behind them as a matter of course."*

6. *"Plantation shutters add value on resale most reliably in period homes — they read as a serious window furnishing decision rather than a temporary fix, and most buyers price that in."*

If a draft sentence feels significantly off the cadence and confidence of those six, rewrite.

## 12. Decision shortcuts — when in doubt

- When in doubt about voice → re-read the six tuning sentences in §11 and rewrite to that rhythm.
- When in doubt about a fact → don't include it. The skill is allowed to be terse.
- When in doubt about taking a position → take it. Neutral feature lists are off-brand.
- When in doubt about a price → don't quote one. Reference `service-no-published-pricing`.
- When in doubt about a regulatory claim → don't make one. Route to Alex.
- When in doubt about a superlative → cut it.
- When in doubt about a metaphor → cut it. We don't do metaphors.
- When in doubt about Australian-vs-American → Australian, every time.
- When in doubt about whether something sounds like an installer or like a marketing person → installer. Always installer.
